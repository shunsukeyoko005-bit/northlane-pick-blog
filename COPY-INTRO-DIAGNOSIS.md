# Blog intro — diagnosis style (required from 2026-07-05)

> **Owner decision 2026-07-05:** Sell the escape, not the product.  
> Readers buy relief from a trap they already feel — not SPF specs in paragraph one.

**Automation:** Read this file on every publish run (with `AUTOMATION.md`, `IMAGES.md`).

---

## Principle

| Weak (feature-first) | Strong (diagnosis-first) |
|----------------------|---------------------------|
| "This sunscreen is lightweight and SPF 50." | "If your neck burns every summer while your face stays fine, you are treating symptoms, not the pattern." |
| "Here is how to layer serums." | "Most people stack products in the wrong order for years and wonder why nothing changes." |
| Explains what the product **does** | Names the trap they are **already in**, then offers the exit |

The article body still educates (ingredients, steps, picks). The intro **diagnoses** before it teaches.

---

## Required structure (first section after `# Title`)

Place **immediately after the H1** and **before** the first `##` heading. Total **3–5 short paragraphs** (or 2 paragraphs + 1 optional question). US English.

### Block A — Mirror the trap (1–2 sentences)

Name a **specific, familiar pain** for this topic. No product names yet.

- Sunburn only on neck/hands
- Buying SPF every year but still burning on vacation
- Routine feels complicated so they skip steps
- Sensitive skin that stings after "gentle" products

### Block B — Cost of unchanged (1 sentence)

What **stays the same** if they change nothing for ~1 season or ~1 year. Use time, confidence, or skin — not guilt-tripping.

Examples:
- "Another summer of covering up photos you would rather share."
- "Another year of guessing which step comes first."

### Block C — Bridge (1 sentence)

What **this article** does: names the pattern + gives a simple exit. Not a sales pitch.

Example:
- "This guide names the spots and order that actually matter for [topic] — without adding clutter to your shelf."

### Optional Block D — One diagnosis question (max 1)

A single question they cannot dodge. Do **not** stack three questions (Pinterest pin copy may; blog intro does not).

Example:
- "When was the last time you reapplied SPF after swimming — not just before you left the house?"

## Topic → trap map (pick one per article)

| Cluster / topic | Trap to diagnose |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Sunscreen / SPF | Forgetting non-face skin; reapply myth; vacation regret |
| Routine order | Steps done out of order for years; product hoarding |
| Sensitive skin | Stripping cleanser + "gentle" products that still sting |
| Toner / optional steps | Buying steps because charts say so, not because skin needs them |
| Niacinamide / vitamin C | Layering conflicts; irritation from stacking actives |
| Night routine | Skipping PM care because mornings feel hard enough |
| Oily / combination | Mattifying everything and breaking the barrier |

Match the backlog title. If the title is "Sunscreen Expiration", diagnose **keeping a 3-year-old bottle** — not generic summer burn.

---

## Body rules (unchanged + sequencing)

1. **Intro = diagnosis** (this file).
2. **`##` sections = education** — how-to, comparisons, tables.
3. **Product / affiliate links** appear after the reader understands the trap (mid-article or later). First affiliate link **not** in Block A–C.
4. Affiliate disclosure stays at top (before H1 block per existing style).
5. No prices. "Check current price on Amazon."
6. No fabricated ASINs.

---

## Tone guardrails

**Do:**
- Calm, specific, US skincare reader
- "You might notice…" / "A common pattern is…"
- Tie pain to **behavior**, not character

**Do not:**
- Medical claims or cure language
- Shame ("you failed your skin")
- Fake countdown urgency
- Fear without a constructive exit in the same article

## Example (full intro only)

```markdown
# SPF Before Outdoor Workouts: What Holds Up to Sweat

*As an Amazon Associate…*

Most people apply SPF once before a run and assume sweat means they are protected. A common pattern is a red forehead and burned shoulders after a 90-minute outdoor workout — even when you "put sunscreen on."

If nothing changes, you spend another season choosing between skipping outdoor training and dealing with peeling skin that sets you back for weeks.

This article names what actually breaks down when you sweat and which formats are worth carrying — without a 10-step gym bag routine.

When did you last reapply SPF mid-workout instead of only at the car before you started?
```
